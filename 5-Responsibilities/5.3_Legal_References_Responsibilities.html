Legal References: Responsibilities and Exclusions

1. AODA — Integrated Accessibility Standards

O. Reg. 165/16, s. 80.47 — Use of Service Animals and Support Persons

(2) If a person with a disability is accompanied by a guide dog or other service animal, the provider shall ensure that the person is permitted to enter the premises with the animal and to keep the animal with him or her, unless the animal is otherwise excluded by law from the premises.

(3) If a service animal is excluded by law from the premises, the provider shall ensure that other measures are available to enable a person with a disability to obtain, use or benefit from the provider's goods, services or facilities.

2. Ontario Human Rights Code

R.S.O. 1990, c. H.19, s. 1 — Guarantees equal treatment without discrimination because of disability. The duty to accommodate is subject to undue hardship (safety and cost). The OHRC's competing rights policy provides a balancing framework (ohrc.on.ca/en/competing-human-rights).

3. Key Case Law

  • Scott v. Siu (2017 HRTO 1108): Requesting supporting documentation (medical letter) is not discriminatory, especially given the prevalence of fake service animal identifiers purchased online.
  • Robinson-Cooke v. Ontario (2023 HRTO 1133): Requiring certification from an ADI-accredited facility is discriminatory. The legal test is medical necessity, not formal training.
  • Leach v. Ontario (HRTO): A claimant who failed to prove a clear connection between the animal and their disability did not establish service animal status. Functionality, not mere presence, is the test.

4. Educational Settings

PPM 163 — Ontario school boards must create policies balancing the needs of the student requiring the animal, students with allergies or phobias, and staff safety (ontario.ca/document/education-ontario-policy-and-program-direction/policyprogram-memorandum-163).
