1 Ground Response — What to Do When Denied

Prince Edward Island passed a Service Dog Act in November 2024 (SPEI 2024, c 81) — but it's not yet in force. Regulations haven't been drafted yet. For now, only the Human Rights Act (RSPEI 1988, c H-12) protects you. No certification, no registry, no paperwork — self-training counts. It's PEI : be friendly, and you'll likely get in. If refused :

  1. Stay friendly, explain : "This dog is trained to help me with my disability. Under the Human Rights Act, you can't discriminate against me." A calm explanation goes a long way on the Island.
  2. Answer questions : Staff can ask what your dog does for you and if it's trained. No diagnosis disclosure required.
  3. If refused, leave : Escalating rarely helps in Island communities. Leave and file with the PEI Human Rights Commission.

What staff can ask

  • Reasonable : "Is your dog trained to assist you?" and "What task does it perform?"
  • Never : asking about your disability, demanding certification or proof of training

No-pet policies don't apply

  • No-pet clauses unenforceable against service dogs under the HR Act
  • Landlords cannot charge pet fees or refuse tenancy
  • Keep your dog under control at all times

Coming soon : Once the Service Dog Act comes into force (likely late 2026), certified teams will have clear access rights with provincial ID cards. Check back for updates.

2 Legal Framework

The PEI reality : A new Service Dog Act exists but isn't in force yet. For now, only the Human Rights Act protects you. When the Act kicks in, certified teams will have clear, enforceable access rights — similar to Nova Scotia's system.

  • Human Rights Act (RSPEI 1988, c H-12) — Prohibits discrimination based on disability. Disability includes "physical reliance on an assist animal, wheelchair or other remedial device." Covers services, accommodation, employment, housing. This is your current protection.
  • Service Dog Act (SPEI 2024, c 81) — Passed November 29, 2024. Not yet in force (regulations pending). Will create a voluntary certification system with a Registrar, certificates, and ID cards. Certified teams will have the right to enter any public place. Dogs only (no other species). Fine : $500-$3,000. The HR Act prevails in case of conflict (s. 4).

Key case

  • None found — No reported PEI jurisprudence on service animals as of June 2026.

3 Key Definitions

What qualifies (current law — HR Act)

  • Definition : The HR Act does not define "service animal" explicitly. Disability includes reliance on an "assist animal." Self-training counts. No certification required.
  • Dogs only under the future Service Dog Act — but for now, other animals may be covered under the HR Act's general disability protection.
  • No vest, harness, or ID required.

What qualifies (future — Service Dog Act)

  • A dog trained to perform specific tasks for a person with a disability, certified by the Registrar. ID card issued. Dog must wear ID collar/harness in public. (Not yet in force.)

What does NOT qualify

  • ESAs and pets : The HR Act requires a disability-related need. Comfort-by-presence alone is not sufficient.

4 Recourse — How to file a complaint

Under the Human Rights Act (current)

  • PEI Human Rights Commission — no fee, no lawyer required
  • Contact : princeedwardisland.ca/human-rights | 902-368-4180 | toll-free 1-800-240-3445
  • Filing deadline : 12 months from the alleged discrimination

Under the Service Dog Act (future)

  • Once in force, report violations (denied access, interference) to the Service Dog Registrar. Fine : $500-$3,000.

Steps

  1. Document : date, time, location, names, witnesses
  2. Write the business explaining the HR Act and duty to accommodate
  3. File with the PEI Human Rights Commission if unresolved

5 Exceptions & Handler's Responsibility

  • Keep your dog under control at all times
  • Be friendly, explain yourself — on the Island, relationships matter
  • Clean up after your dog in public spaces
  • You are responsible for any damages your dog causes

Possible exceptions

  • Sterile/restricted areas : health centres, food prep areas
  • Direct threat : demonstrated dangerous behaviour, not fear or allergies
  • Undue hardship : accommodation applies up to undue hardship

Key takeaway : For now, the Human Rights Act is your protection — no paperwork, no certification, no fuss. When the Service Dog Act kicks in (late 2026?), getting certified will make access even smoother. In the meantime, be a good community member, keep your dog under control, and file a complaint if refused.
