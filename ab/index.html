1 Ground Response — What to Do When Denied

Alberta's Service Dogs Act is 3 pages. Section 3 prohibits discrimination. Section 4 lets the Minister issue ID cards — the only recognized proof (s. 4(2)). Without one, the law is weak. If refused :

  1. Stay calm, show the card : "This is my service dog. Here is my Alberta Service Dog ID card. Under the Service Dogs Act, I have the right to be here."
  2. No card? : You are still protected by s. 3 of the Act and s. 4 of the Alberta Human Rights Act, but the ID card is the only recognized proof. Without it, expect scrutiny — complaints from non-card holders are routinely dismissed (Neufeld 2025 AHRC 109, Hill 2023 AHRC 119).
  3. If still refused, leave : Do not escalate. In Alberta, confrontation rarely helps. Leave and file a complaint afterward.

What staff can ask

  • With ID card : Show it. Access must be granted. Zero questions.
  • Without ID card : Staff can ask for proof. The Act does not require you to carry a card — but in practice, without it, a complaint will likely be dismissed.

2 Alberta Reality & Case Law

The Alberta reality : The provincial ID card is the only game in town. Section 3 protects everyone on paper, but 5 of 6 recent service dog complaints were dismissed. Get the card.

  • Hill v Britton's Your Independent Grocer (2023 AHRC 119) — Dismissed. Handler did not identify the dog as a service dog, told staff to "call the RCMP." No card = no case.
  • Neufeld v Alberta (2025 AHRC 109) — Dismissed. Denied access to Calgary Court Centre. No provincial ID card. The decision cites s. 4(2) : the card is the only recognized proof.
  • Dugas v West Edmonton Mall (2024 AHRC 56) — Dismissed. Off-leash dog. Private certification not recognized.
  • Boulet v Edmonton Radial Railway (2025 AHRC 78) — Sent to Tribunal (the only survivor). Certified dog with provincial ID card. Conductor asked him to wait for allergy check.

3 How to get an ID card

  • ADI path (AR 59/2017, s. 1(1)(a)) — Trained by an Assistance Dogs International-accredited school.
  • Qualified List path (s. 1(1)(b)) — Trained or tested by an organization on the Minister's Qualified List. Owner-trained dogs can apply for assessment through this route.
  • Provincial ID card (s. 4(1)) — Once qualified, the Minister issues an ID card. This card is the only legally recognized proof.

4 Recourse — How to file a complaint

Under the Service Dogs Act

  • Fine up to $3,000 for discrimination (s. 6(1)).
  • Report to the Service Dog Assessment Team : 780-427-9136, servicedogs@gov.ab.ca

Under the Alberta Human Rights Act

  • File with the Alberta Human Rights Commission : albertahumanrights.ab.ca
  • Warning : 5 of 6 recent service dog complaints were dismissed. The Commission screens cases early — without a provincial ID card, your complaint has little chance.

Steps to take

  1. Try an informal step first : A letter to the business explaining s. 3 of the Act may resolve the issue without formal proceedings.
  2. Contact the Service Dog Assessment Team if the business discriminated against a qualified team.
  3. File with the Alberta Human Rights Commission if the informal step fails.
  4. Document your case : date, time, location, names, witnesses, description.

5 Exceptions & Handler's Responsibility

  • Keep your dog under control at all times — off-leash = no protection (Dugas 2024 AHRC 56)
  • If you do not control your dog, s. 3 protection does not apply (s. 3(4))
  • Identify your animal as a service dog — silently expecting access gets your complaint dismissed (Hill 2023 AHRC 119)
  • Clean up after your dog in public spaces
  • You are responsible for any damages your dog causes

Possible exceptions

  • Sterile areas : Operating rooms, burn units — access restricted for health reasons
  • Direct threat : Real and demonstrated dangerous behaviour, not a hypothetical fear
  • Restricted areas : Food preparation areas, other non-public zones — but the main public space must remain accessible

Alberta's law is simple : get the provincial ID card, show it, and you get in. Without it, you are in a legal gray zone where 5 out of 6 complainants lost.
