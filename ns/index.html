1 Ground Response — What to Do When Denied

Nova Scotia has a voluntary certification system under the Service Dog Act (SNS 2016, c 4) — get the provincial ID card and you have ironclad access rights. But this is Nova Scotia. Even without the card, if you explain yourself calmly and smile, you'll probably get in. The Human Rights Act backs you up either way. Two scenarios :

If you are certified (have the provincial ID card)

  1. Show your card : "This is my certified service dog. Under the Service Dog Act, you must allow us access." Your ID card is proof (s. 14).
  2. Your dog must wear its ID collar/harness in public (s. 15(1)). This is a legal requirement for certified teams.
  3. Access cannot be denied : It is an offence to interfere with a certified team's right of entry (s. 11(2)), fine $500–$3,000 (s. 18).

If you are not certified

  1. Smile and answer questions : Friendly small talk goes a long way in Nova Scotia. Explain that your dog is trained to help you. Most people will nod and let you in.
  2. If refused, don't push it : The Human Rights Act protects you, but escalating on site is not the NS way. Leave and file a complaint afterward.

What staff can ask

  • Certified team : Staff can ask to see your provincial ID card (s. 14). Show it, access granted.
  • Non-certified : Friendly conversation. "What does your dog do for you?" is the usual approach. Answer with a smile. No formal "two questions" rule.

2 Legal Framework

The Nova Scotia reality : Two tracks. Certified teams have clear, enforceable access rights under the Service Dog Act. Non-certified handlers must rely on the Human Rights Act — complaint-driven, slower, less certain. Certification is voluntary but strongly recommended.

  • Service Dog Act (SNS 2016, c 4) — Voluntary certification system. Certified teams have the right to enter any place the public can access (s. 11(1)). No person shall interfere with that right (s. 11(2)). Denying tenancy based on a service dog is prohibited (s. 12). Handler must show certificate/ID on request (s. 14). Dog must wear ID collar/harness in public (s. 15). Fine : $500–$3,000 for violations (s. 18).
  • NS Reg 94/2018 (Service Dogs Regulations) — Two certification paths : ADI/IGDF accredited training OR private training + public safety assessment. Certificate valid 2 years. Renewal requires reassessment. No application fee, but assessment fee for privately trained dogs.
  • Human Rights Act (RSNS 1989, c 214) — Prohibits disability discrimination. References "service dog as defined in the Service Dog Act." Covers non-certified handlers. Complaint-based enforcement.

Key case

  • None found — No reported NS jurisprudence on service dog access or certification disputes as of June 2026.

3 Key Definitions

Certified service dog team

  • Path 1 — Accredited school : Dog trained by an ADI or IGDF-accredited school. Submit application + written confirmation from school + photo. No assessment required.
  • Path 2 — Private training : Dog trained by you or a non-accredited trainer. Must pass a public safety assessment by a designated assessor. Assessment fee applies.
  • Certificate valid 2 years. Renewal requires passing another assessment within 90 days.
  • Out-of-province teams with valid certification can get a temporary NS certificate.

Non-certified service dog

  • Protected by the Human Rights Act only. Definition follows the Service Dog Act: a dog trained to perform physical tasks for a person with a disability.
  • No vest, harness, or ID required — but without certification, access is not guaranteed under the Service Dog Act.

What does NOT qualify

  • ESAs : The Act requires task-specific training. Comfort-by-presence alone does not qualify.
  • Pets : Not protected.

4 Recourse — How to file a complaint

Under the Service Dog Act (certified teams)

  • Report violations to the Service Dog Program — denied access, denied tenancy, or interference
  • Fine : $500–$3,000 per offence (s. 18)
  • Contact : novascotia.ca/service-dog-program/

Under the Human Rights Act (non-certified)

  • Nova Scotia Human Rights Commission — no fee, no lawyer required
  • Contact : humanrights.novascotia.ca | 902-424-4111 | toll-free 1-877-269-7699

Steps

  1. Document : date, time, location, business, names, witnesses, photos
  2. Certified : Report directly to the Service Dog Program for Act violations
  3. Non-certified : Write the business explaining the HR Act, then file with the Commission if unresolved

5 Exceptions & Handler's Responsibility

  • Keep your dog under control at all times — calm, focused, responsive
  • Certified : wear the ID collar/harness whenever in public (s. 15(1))
  • Show your certificate when asked (s. 14) — refusing to show it weakens your right of access
  • Clean up after your dog in public spaces
  • You are responsible for any damages your dog causes
  • Threatening behaviour : The Registrar can cancel a certificate if the dog acts in a manner that threatens safety (Reg 94/2018, s. 22)

Possible exceptions

  • Sterile/restricted areas : operating rooms, burn units, food prep behind the counter
  • Direct threat : demonstrated dangerous behaviour, not fear or allergies
  • Undue hardship : in housing and employment, accommodation applies up to undue hardship

Key takeaway : Get certified. NS's Service Dog Act gives you the clearest access rights in Atlantic Canada — show your ID card, keep the collar on, and the law is on your side. Without certification, your protection is complaint-based under the Human Rights Act.
