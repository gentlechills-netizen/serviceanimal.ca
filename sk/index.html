1 Ground Response — What to Do When Denied

Protection comes from the Human Rights Code, 2018 (SS 2018, c S-24.2), which prohibits disability discrimination in services, accommodation, and employment. Your animal qualifies if trained to perform specific tasks for a disability — self-training counts.

  1. Stay calm, state your rights : "This is a service animal trained to assist me with a disability. Under The Saskatchewan Human Rights Code, you cannot discriminate against me because of my reliance on a service animal."
  2. Explain what the animal does : Be ready to describe the task it's trained to perform (e.g., "retrieves items I drop," "alerts me to medical episodes"). You do not have to disclose your diagnosis.
  3. No proof required : Saskatchewan has no certification, no ID card, no registry. Online registries like "Official Service Dog Registry" or "Service Dogs America" have no legal standing in Canada (Roberts Properties v O.S., 2018 SKORT 177). Fees are a scam.
  4. If still refused, leave and file : Document and file a complaint with the Human Rights Commission.

What staff can ask

  • Reasonable : "What task is your animal trained to perform?" and "Is it trained to assist with a disability?"
  • Never allowed : "What's your disability?", "Show certification", "Prove it", vaccination or licensing proof

No-pet policies don't apply

  • A genuine service animal overrides a no-pet policy (Roberts Properties, 2018 SKORT 177)
  • Landlords cannot refuse tenancy or charge pet fees for a service animal
  • If the animal is not under control, access may be refused

2 Legal Framework

The Saskatchewan reality : No specific access law, no certification, no registry. The Human Rights Code is your only tool for access. The Animal Protection Act protects against interference — not access. The HR Commission policy guides what counts as a service animal.

  • Saskatchewan Human Rights Code, 2018 (SS 2018, c S-24.2) — Section 12 prohibits discrimination in accommodation, services, and facilities on the basis of disability. Disability includes "physical reliance on a service animal, wheelchair or other remedial appliance or device" (s. 2(1)(d)(viii)).
  • Animal Protection Act, 2018 (SS 2018, c A-21.2) — Defines "service animal" : an animal trained to be used by a person with a disability.
  • Human Rights Commission policy : The SK HR Commission has a detailed service animal policy listing recognized types (guide dogs, hearing dogs, mobility dogs, psychiatric service animals, seizure response dogs, social signal dogs) and requiring that training go beyond normal pet behaviour.

Key case

  • Roberts Properties Inc. v O.S. (2018 SKORT 177) — Tenant registered dog with US-based "Official Service Dog Registry." Hearing officer found the training insufficient. Order for possession granted. Confirms : online "certification" from US registries has no legal standing in Saskatchewan. A genuine service animal must be trained beyond what a typical pet does.

3 Key Definitions

What qualifies

  • Definition (Animal Protection Act) : "An animal that is trained to be used by a person with a disability for reasons relating to his or her disability."
  • Training matters : The HR Commission policy requires training for specific tasks beyond what a normal pet does. Retrieving dropped items may count if genuinely task-trained, but won't if it's just natural breed behaviour.
  • Self-training is valid : No accredited organization required.
  • No vest, harness, or ID required : all optional.

Who is protected

  • Any person with a disability relying on a trained service animal — physical, sensory, mental health, neurological, intellectual.
  • No certification needed. No registry exists in Saskatchewan.

What does NOT qualify

  • ESAs and pets : The definition requires training for specific tasks. Comfort-by-presence alone does not qualify.
  • Online registry dogs : US-based online registrations (Official Service Dog Registry, Service Dogs America) carry no legal weight in Saskatchewan (Roberts Properties, 2018).

4 Recourse — How to file a complaint

Under the Human Rights Code

  • Saskatchewan Human Rights Commission — no fee, no lawyer required. Handles discrimination in services, accommodation, and employment.
  • Contact : saskatchewanhumanrights.ca | 306-933-5952 | toll-free 1-800-667-8577

Under the Animal Protection Act

  • Someone harms or interferes with your service animal? Report to local police or the Ministry of Agriculture (animal protection services). Provincial offence.

Steps

  1. Document : date, time, location, business, names, witnesses, photos
  2. Write the business explaining the HR Code and their obligation to accommodate
  3. File with the SK Human Rights Commission if unresolved

5 Exceptions & Handler's Responsibility

  • Keep your animal under control : calm, focused, responsive. Uncontrolled behaviour = grounds for refusal
  • Training must be real : Your animal must perform specific trained tasks beyond normal pet behaviour. Roberts (2018) is the warning — natural breed instincts won't cut it
  • Clean up after your animal in public spaces
  • You are responsible for any damages your animal causes

Possible exceptions

  • Sterile/restricted areas : operating rooms, burn units, food prep areas
  • Direct threat : demonstrated dangerous behaviour, not fear or allergies
  • Undue hardship : in housing and employment, accommodation applies up to undue hardship

Key takeaway : No certification, no registry. Your animal needs real task training — not an online registration. Know what your animal does, keep it under control, and file a complaint if refused.
