1 On the Ground — What to Do When Denied Access

You are legally entitled to enter any public place with your service animal in Ontario. If you are denied access, stay calm and follow these steps:

  1. State your right: "This is a service animal required for my disability. Under the Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA) and the Ontario Human Rights Code, you must allow me entry on presentation of my Service Animal Letter."
  2. Ask for a manager: If the employee refuses, politely ask to speak with the manager or person in charge. Lower-level staff often do not know the law.
  3. Do not leave voluntarily: You have the right to be there. If they insist you leave, tell them you will file a human rights complaint. Say: "I will be filing a complaint with the Human Rights Tribunal of Ontario. Your business may face legal consequences."
  4. Document everything: Write down the date, time, location, names of staff involved, and what was said. Take notes on your phone immediately.
  5. Leave only if necessary: If the situation escalates or you feel unsafe, leave and file a complaint afterward. Your safety comes first.

Key Points to Remember

  • No certification or identification card is legally required for your service animal in Ontario.
  • Staff may request a letter from a regulated health professional confirming the animal is required for a disability, even if the animal wears a visible identifier (O. Reg. 191/11, s. 80.45; Scott v. Siu, 2017 HRTO 627). They cannot ask about your disability, demand a training certificate, or require a task demonstration (Allarie v. Rouble, 2010 HRTO 61; Robinson-Cooke v. Ontario, 2023 HRTO 1133).
  • They cannot ask for proof of disability, medical documents, dog vaccination status, or a municipal licence.
  • Service animals must be under control (leash or harness) and housebroken.
  • If your animal is out of control or not housebroken, you may be asked to leave (O. Reg. 191/11, s. 80.47(5); HR Code s. 17(1); Dog Owners' Liability Act).

2 Legal Basis

Service animal access in Ontario is protected by two main laws:

Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA)

The Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act, 2005 (AODA) and its Integrated Accessibility Standards Regulation (IASR) require all businesses and public organizations to accommodate service animals. Under Ontario Regulation 191/11, section 80.47, if a person with a disability is accompanied by a service animal, the provider must permit the person to enter the premises with the animal and keep it with them.

Ontario Human Rights Code

The Human Rights Code (R.S.O. 1990, c. H.19) prohibits discrimination based on disability (s. 1). The definition of disability is broad and protects both visible and invisible conditions, including mental health, cognitive, and psychiatric disabilities (s. 10). Internal business policies cannot override the Code (s. 47(2)). A person who contravenes the Code is liable to a fine of up to $25,000 (s. 46.2(1)).

Key Legal References

  • AODA: S.O. 2005, c. 11 — Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act
  • Service Animals (IASR): O. Reg. 191/11, s. 80.45-80.47 — Service animal definition, access rights and exclusions
  • Human Rights Code: R.S.O. 1990, c. H.19 — ss. 1, 10, 29, 46.2
  • Health Protection Act: O. Reg. 493/17, s. 14 — Service dogs in food premises
  • Penalty: Fine up to $25,000 for contravention (OHRC s. 46.2(1))

3 Key Definitions

Who is protected

Any person with a disability (physical, mental, intellectual, or sensory) who relies on a service animal for tasks related to their disability is protected under Ontario law.

What qualifies as a service animal

Ontario law defines a service animal as an animal that is:

  • Easily identifiable as a service animal (e.g., wearing a vest, harness, or identification tag); or
  • Documented by a regulated health professional as required for a disability.

Note: Even if the animal wears a vest or harness, a business owner may still request a medical letter from one of the following regulated health professionals: physician, nurse, psychologist, occupational therapist, physiotherapist, chiropractor, optometrist, audiologist/speech-language pathologist, or registered psychotherapist (O. Reg. 191/11, s. 80.45(4)b).

Not covered

  • Emotional support animals (ESAs) are not service animals under Ontario law
  • Therapy animals used in clinical settings
  • Pets

4 Recourse — Filing a Complaint

If you were denied access or treated unfairly because of your service animal, you can file a complaint.

Human Rights Tribunal of Ontario (HRTO)

File an application under the Human Rights Code. There is no fee to file.

  • Deadline: You have 1 year from the incident to file an application.
  • How to file: Visit tribunalsontario.ca/hrto and complete an Application form.
  • What you can get: The Tribunal can order an apology, compensation for injury to dignity, and changes to the business's policies.

Before filing — send a formal notice

Sending a letter to the business before filing often resolves the issue quickly. Use our email template or formal letter template.

What to include in your complaint

  • Date, time, and location of the incident
  • Names of the people who denied you access
  • Any witnesses or their contact information
  • A description of what happened, what was said
  • Any documentation (emails, medical letters, photos)

5 Exceptions & Handler's Responsibility

  • Keep your animal under control at all times (leash, harness, or voice control)
  • Ensure your animal is housebroken
  • You are responsible for any damage your animal causes
  • If your animal is out of control and you do not regain control, you may be asked to leave

Exceptions

  • Food premises: Service animals are generally allowed in restaurants, but not behind counters or in food preparation areas (O. Reg. 493/17, s. 14)
  • Health facilities: Service animals may be restricted in operating rooms, burn units, and sterile areas
  • Under control: If the animal poses a direct threat to health or safety (not speculation, but real evidence), access may be limited

Note on enforcement

The AODA does not have specific fines for denying service animal access. However, the Human Rights Code allows the Tribunal to award compensation for injury to dignity, feelings, and self-respect — typically up to $35,000 (and sometimes more) according to Tribunal jurisprudence. The threat of a human rights complaint is often enough to resolve access issues quickly.
